Most SEO guides tell you to "write for humans, not robots." That's good advice — but incomplete.

The real goal is to write content that **satisfies search intent** while being technically sound enough for Google to surface it. Here's how I approach it after writing 500+ SEO articles.

## 1. Start with Intent, Not Just Keywords

Before you write a single word, ask: **what does the person typing this query actually want?**

- "Best SEO tools" → they want a list with comparisons
- "How to do keyword research" → they want a step-by-step process
- "SEMrush vs Ahrefs" → they want a direct comparison

Match the format and depth to the intent. A listicle for navigational queries. A long-form guide for informational ones.

## 2. Structure First, Write Second

Outline your H2s before writing. Each heading should answer a specific sub-question your reader has. Think of your article as a conversation — anticipate what they'll ask next.

Good structure:
- Intro (what and why)
- H2s covering each angle
- FAQ section (targets featured snippets)
- Conclusion with CTA

## 3. The 10% Rule for Keyword Density

Forget "keyword density percentages." Instead: use your primary keyword naturally in:
- Title tag
- First 100 words
- One H2
- Last paragraph
- Meta description

Then use semantic variations throughout. Google understands context.

## 4. Internal Linking is Underrated

Every article should link to 2–3 related pieces on the site. This passes authority, improves crawlability, and keeps readers on-site longer — all positive signals.

## 5. Update, Don't Just Create

A 2-year-old article refreshed with current data and new sections often outperforms a brand-new piece. Google rewards freshness. I schedule quarterly reviews of top-performing content.
